Recurrent UTIs

A recurrent urinary tract infection (UTI) is defined as two or more UTIs within 6 months, or three or more within a year. UTIs are far more common in women than men, largely due to anatomy — the urethra is shorter and bacteria have less distance to travel. Symptoms include burning with urination, urgency, frequency, and sometimes blood in the urine. In postmenopausal women, declining estrogen levels lead to changes in vaginal and urethral tissue that significantly increase susceptibility. Most cases are managed with targeted antibiotics, but prevention strategies — including vaginal estrogen, hydration habits, and behavioral changes — are equally important.

Common Medications

  • Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (Bactrim/Septra) — commonly used for acute UTI treatment
  • Nitrofurantoin (Macrobid) — effective for bladder infections; not for kidney infections
  • Ciprofloxacin (Cipro) — broad-spectrum; reserved for complicated or resistant cases
  • Fosfomycin (Monurol) — single-dose option; good for resistant organisms
  • Methenamine hippurate (Hiprex) — urinary antiseptic used for long-term prevention without antibiotics
  • Vaginal estrogen (Vagifem, Estrace, Premarin cream) — first-line prevention in postmenopausal women; not a systemic hormone therapy
  • Low-dose prophylactic antibiotics — taken daily or post-intercourse for prevention in high-frequency recurrence

Before Your Visit

Preparing these items will help us make the most of your appointment time.

  • Bring or describe results of any prior urine cultures — knowing which bacteria and which antibiotics worked helps guide treatment.
  • Note the frequency of infections over the past year and any patterns (e.g., associated with intercourse).
  • List all current medications including any supplements (cranberry, D-mannose, probiotics).
  • For postmenopausal women: discuss your history with vaginal dryness or discomfort — it's often related and treatable.
  • Note any history of kidney infections, hospitalizations, or IV antibiotics for UTIs.

After Your Visit

Key next steps and what to focus on.

  • Take the full antibiotic course even if symptoms resolve early.
  • Drink at least 6–8 glasses of water per day — diluting urine is one of the simplest preventive measures.
  • Urinate soon after sexual intercourse if UTIs are associated with sex.
  • If prescribed vaginal estrogen: give it 3 months to show benefit — it works gradually.
  • If prescribed preventive antibiotics: take them exactly as directed and report any side effects.

When to Call the Office

Call us at (206) 223-6772 if you experience any of the following:

  • Fever above 101°F, chills, or back/flank pain with UTI symptoms — this may indicate a kidney infection.
  • Symptoms not improving within 48–72 hours of starting antibiotics.
  • Blood in the urine — even if you've had UTIs before, this always warrants evaluation.
  • Three or more UTIs in a year if we haven't discussed a prevention strategy yet.
