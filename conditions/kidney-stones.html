Kidney Stones

Kidney stones are hard mineral deposits that form inside your kidneys. They can cause intense pain in your back or side, nausea, and blood in the urine — often with little warning. Most stones pass on their own with increased fluid intake, but larger stones may require a procedure to break them up or remove them. The good news: with the right diet and hydration, many patients can significantly reduce their risk of future stones.

Non-invasive shock waves break stones into small fragments that pass naturally through urine — no incision required.

A temporary tube placed between your kidney and bladder to keep urine flowing — commonly used after stone procedures.

Common Medications

  • Potassium citrate — alkalinizes urine; reduces calcium oxalate and uric acid stones
  • Allopurinol — lowers uric acid; used for uric acid or calcium oxalate stones
  • Hydrochlorothiazide / Chlorthalidone — reduces calcium in urine for calcium stone formers
  • Acetohydroxamic acid — for struvite (infection) stones
  • Tiopronin (Thiola) / Penicillamine — for cystine stones
  • Tamsulosin (Flomax) — alpha-blocker to help small stones pass

Before Your Visit

Preparing these items will help us make the most of your appointment time.

  • Bring any prior stone analysis results (composition) if you have them.
  • Note how many stone episodes you've had and roughly when.
  • Write down your typical daily fluid intake and any dietary habits (high salt, meat, or oxalate foods).
  • Bring a list of all current medications and supplements.
  • If you have imaging (CT or ultrasound) from another facility, bring a disc or have it sent electronically.

After Your Visit

Key next steps and what to focus on.

  • Drink enough fluid to produce at least 2–2.5 liters of urine per day (urine should be pale yellow).
  • Follow any dietary changes discussed — these are often more powerful than medications.
  • If a stent was placed, avoid strenuous activity until it is removed.
  • Strain your urine to catch any stone fragments and bring them in for analysis if instructed.
  • Take all prescribed medications as directed and complete the full course of any antibiotics.

When to Call the Office

Call us at (206) 223-6772 if you experience any of the following:

  • Fever above 101°F (38.3°C) with flank pain — this may indicate a kidney infection requiring urgent care.
  • Uncontrolled pain not responding to prescribed medications.
  • Inability to keep down fluids due to vomiting.
  • No urine output for more than 8 hours.
  • Stent-related concerns: severe pain, visible stent movement, or inability to remove stent if on a string.
